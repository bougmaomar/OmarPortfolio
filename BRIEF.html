The strategy — read this first

The why is a compass, not a headline.

Sinek built "start with why" by studying Apple, Martin Luther King, the Wright Brothers — movements and giant brands with the airtime to lead with belief. A one-person shop with no track record yet doesn't have that airtime. Lead a homepage with a poetic belief statement and a busy café or clinic owner hears: "nice words, but can you build the thing, how much, and have you done it before?"

So the why gets demoted, not deleted. It lives in three quiet places: in my own head (so I stay motivated and know who to say no to), in how I talk on a call, and as one honest line late on the page. A brief is internal — that's the right place for the why to be loud. A homepage is not.

What actually converts for local B2B services is the boring stuff, in this order:

  1. A crisp statement of what I do and for whom
  2. Proof — experience and work
  3. Trust signals — fixed quotes, plain language, you own it
  4. How to reach me
Sinek is the steering wheel. Voss is the engine. The discovery questions, naming the client's fears out loud, and framing value as "how many bookings are you losing" — that's the part that actually moves people from "maybe" to "yes." The belief lands harder as a closing note than as an opening claim.

Discovery call — quick reference

  • What made you reach out now?
  • What's the hardest part of running this, day to day?
  • What would have to change for this to feel worth it to you?
  • Label: "It sounds like the real problem isn't the website — it's that people can't find you."
  • Value frame: "Roughly how many bookings do you think you lose each month to this?"

Keep summarizing their world back until they say "that's right." Let "no" stay safe. Listen for the one detail that reshapes everything.

Lead with the why on calls. Lead with clarity on the page.
